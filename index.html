Keith Hume Fraser Reserve · 95 Swansea Rd, Montrose

Four pitches. Two grounds. One home for local sport.

Lilydale Montrose United has developed a master plan to transform the reserve's tired, poorly drained playing fields into a four-pitch football precinct and two full-size community cricket grounds — with a new pavilion, viewing deck and proper parking — while leaving the bushland untouched — and wants feedback from football families, cricketers and neighbours alike.

The club

A growing club that has outgrown its home

Lilydale Montrose United fields approximately 430 players in 2026, and has been growing at 5–10% a year across junior, senior, men's and women's football.

That growth has already overflowed the reserve: the club regularly hires an additional ground on Quarry Rd, Lilydale just to fit preseason training and season fixtures. Every hired hour is demand that Keith Hume Fraser Reserve can't currently meet — and it's the clearest evidence for why two pitches need to become four. The master plan brings the club's football home to one ground, with capacity for the growth that's already happening.

More about the club at lilydalemontrose.football.

The plan

What's proposed

All four pitches sit on one alignment on the Swansea Rd side of the watercourse, in two paired precincts. In winter each pair hosts football; in summer each precinct becomes one continuous cricket ground with a concrete wicket at its centre.

The pitches

Pitch 2 at 105 × 68 m and Pitch 3 at 100 × 68 m are the main grounds, aiming for 200 lux match lighting; Pitch 1 (88 × 60 m) and Pitch 4 (75 × 56.4 m) support them at 100 lux training/junior standard. Every goal has a minimum 4 m runoff, with fences pulled tight specifically to save the trees along Swansea Rd.

The pavilion

A 70 × 25 m building between the main pitches on a 3 m podium: six player change rooms, two referee rooms, first aid, and a canteen serving both grounds — topped by a rooftop viewing area sighting all four pitches, and a 20 × 20 m open deck at its northern end for outdoor table viewing. Modular build, following the award-recognised Aspendale Gardens pavilion model.

The waterways

The main watercourse is not moved at all. The only channel work in the whole scheme is a ~105 m naturalised realignment of the upper Montrose drain — gentle meanders replacing today's awkward right-angle jog, arguably leaving the channel better than it is now.

The bushland

The steep north corner stays exactly as it is: untouched bushland reserve. The car park sits on the already-disturbed old tennis ground, roughly 12 m off the nearest neighbour with the strip kept as vegetation.

Cricket

Two proper grounds — and a safety fix that's overdue

The cricket sizes are protected in the design: a 110 m boundary on the south ground and 100 m on the north. The football geometry was made to fit around the cricket, not the other way round.

The plan also fixes something the current ground gets badly wrong. The existing northern wicket was surveyed at a 65.5° bearing — which points its south-western batter directly into the setting summer sun, in the final hour of every 1pm–7pm fixture. That's the exact situation that halted an international match at Napier's McLean Park on safety grounds. The new layout eliminates it: the north ground's wicket is engineered to a fully compliant 326° bearing, and the south ground's to within 4° of the guidance window — both keeping the low evening sun well clear of the batters' eyes, with the treed drain corridor screening the western horizon.

Put in the terms that actually matter for a ground that plays 1pm–7pm in summer: every wicket in the new plan keeps the low evening sun at least 56° from every batter's sight line; on the existing ground the best wicket allows it within 30°, and the worst points a batter directly into it.

The plan also holds a pathway to full guidance compliance on both grounds. The south ground's wicket (currently 321°, four degrees outside the window) can rotate to a compliant 325° with about two metres more room between Pitches 1 and 2 — achieved either by shifting Pitch 1 slightly further west once the full detailed site survey confirms the room is there, or, if the survey doesn't allow it, by reducing Pitch 1's width from 60 m to 58 m. Either way this is Stage 3 work on the southern ground — it has no effect on Stages 1 and 2 — and the aim is firm: two fully compliant cricket grounds once the detailed survey is complete.

There's a second safety reason for how the wickets are placed, and it's a sombre one. Most shared grounds — including the reserve's existing ovals — carry their concrete wicket in the middle of the playing field under a seasonal cover, and over the years our players have taken hard falls onto it, including a few broken ankles. In July 2026 a young footballer tragically died at Lalor Recreation Reserve after striking his head on the covered concrete wicket in the middle of the ground, and councils across Victoria are now reviewing every covered in-field wicket. This plan removes the hazard instead of covering it: the concrete slabs sit between the pitches, never within one — at least a metre outside every pitch line, with the team shelters standing over them all winter. No football is played on or across concrete anywhere in the layout.

Access & parking

~220 spaces, zero new road connections

The old Montrose Terrace tennis club entrance at the reserve's south corner becomes the main car park access — reusing the existing crossover, its turning lanes, and the existing drain culvert. Only 75 m of new driveway is needed.

The ~6,200 m² car park sits on the former tennis ground, comfortably above the 120–180 spaces a venue this size typically needs at peak, with room in reserve. Both existing Swansea Rd entrances keep operating unchanged, and the reverse vending machine in the central car park stays exactly where it is. Match-day traffic parks within 100 m of the gate without crossing pedestrian routes between the pitches.

Delivery

Three stages, benchmarked on a proven project

The City of Kingston delivered almost exactly this project at Aspendale Gardens — four football pitches, two cricket ovals and a modular pavilion — jointly funded roughly 50/50 with the Victorian Government (~$9.1M total). This plan follows the same staged, co-funded model.

Questions

Straight answers

Will the watercourse be affected?

Barely. The main watercourse through the reserve is not moved at all, and the lower Montrose drain stays exactly on its existing course. The single piece of channel work is a ~105 m naturalised realignment of the upper drain — designed with gentle meanders, replacing a right-angle jog — which requires Melbourne Water consent and is deliberately modest.

What about trees along Swansea Rd?

Tree saving drove the fence design: every behind-goal fence sits at the 4 m minimum specifically to pull the fenced footprint off the treed frontage — roughly 860 m² less clearing than earlier versions of this same plan. The steep north corner stays as untouched bushland.

Will lighting affect nearby homes?

Light spill toward the Swansea Rd dwellings and the northern bushland is a design criterion from the first layout, not an afterthought. The lighting serves winter football only — cricket is a summer daylight game and doesn't use it — and only the two main pitches carry the 200 lux competition standard.

Will sport stop during construction?

No — that's what the staging is for. Stage 1 builds the new north ground while the existing club rooms and pitches keep operating; only after the pavilion exists does Stage 3 rebuild the top ground. Aspendale Gardens ran the same sequence successfully.

How would it be paid for?

On the Aspendale model: a state/council partnership (theirs split roughly $4.5M / $4.6M). Each stage is a smaller, independently useful ask, spread across budget years, with the pavilion's open deck already built in as a cost-management measure.

Who developed this plan?

Lilydale Montrose United FC — with every dimension verified: pitch runoffs, cricket boundary buffers, waterway offsets and boundary clearances are all checked in a survey-grade coordinate model (MGA55), issued alongside the drawings and ready for council's surveyors. The plan is now out for feedback from the community and from Montrose Cricket Club.
